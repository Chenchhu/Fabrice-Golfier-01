A B S T R A C T


Fracture networks contribute to the differential weathering of fractured bedrock at multiple scales. The influence of competing fracture networks is discussed in light of hydrogeochemical conditions to predict the dissolution pattern resulting from spheroidal weathering. The investigation of the dimensionless Péclet (Pe) and Damköhler (Da)numbers on a simple case of a dual-scale fracture network and its application to three fracture network weathering reveals the influence of fluid flow and reaction rate conditions on the dissolution patterns.

The conceptual model was numerically validated and compared with observations on lateritic nickel deposits in New Caledonia. A fracture network will contribute to boulder development if the associated fluid flow regime reaches a Pe threshold of around 10. On the other hand,predominant diffusion will inhibit the fracture set in favour of a higher permeability fracture network. The Da drives,for its part,the width of the dissolution halo. Owing to the dependency of the fluid flow regime on the fracture network characteristics, scaling laws were established to estimate the size of the boulders at different scales by predicting the fracture contribution as a function of their Pe .

1. Introduction


Located at the base of the Critical Zone, the regolith consists of weathered material blanketing unweathered rock. Regolith formation results from progressive alteration of the bedrock by downward percolating supergene waters; the process is controlled by the permeability and heterogeneities such as fractures acting as flow barriers or drains (Favier et al., 2022; Iseppi et al., 2018; Munday et al., 2003). Weathering processes occurring in the subsurface are essential in the flux dynamics across the earth-atmosphere interface. They were thus described and modeled to understand better the resulting vertical distribution of chemical elements (Brantley and Lebedeva, 2011). However, the complex distribution of structural heterogeneities leads to an uneven dissolution and rough weathering front at the bedrock-saprolite interface, and hence an irregular surface and thickness of the regolith (Lebedeva and Brantley, 2017; Navarre-Sitchler and Brantley, 2007). As a result, in supergene Ni-Co deposits, such heterogeneous regolith distribution implies an uneven Ni-grade distribution, which is still not understood (Cathelineau et al., 2016; Myagkiy et al., 2019; Teitler et al., 2019, 2022). Furthermore, weathering also affects rock mechanical properties, notably their hardness, with important implications for mining activities. Hence, an appropriate evaluation of spatial heterogeneities in the regolith is critical for mineral resource development and production in the mining industry.

Boulders, often associated with spheroidal, concentric or onion-skin weathering (Fletcher et al., 2006; Md Dan et al., 2015), are common weathering heterogeneities encountered at the bedrock-saprolite interface. Boulders typically consist of a weathered outer rim and a mostly unweathered core. In the most weathered cases, they are usually shelled by a thin saprolite layer surrounded by laterite (Md Dan et al., 2016; Ollier, 2010; Patino et al., 2003). This chemical dissolution process is usually accompanied by spheroidal fracturing that forms rindlets or "onion-skins" (Fletcher et al., 2006; Goodfellow and Hilley, 2022). In this work, however, we will only investigate weathering rind formation (i.e. the chemical dissolution) and not consider the weathering-induced fracturing (i.e., the hydromechanical coupling). The boulder development occurs on various rock types, including gneiss, schist, andesite, sandstone, granites, basalts, and peridotites. It is considered a common mechanism initiating the transformation of bedrock to saprolite (R. Fletcher et al., 2006) and resulting from differential weathering (Sarracino et al., 1987). Weathering starts from a fracture network delimiting bedrock blocks. Then, the weathering front progressively develops inwards, creating boulders (Hirata et al., 2017; Navarre-Sitchler et al., 2011; Sarracino et al., 1987). At the scale of the fracture network, a focus has been made on the balance between chemical weathering and erosion rates to predict the block size distribution (Brantley et al., 2011; Fletcher and Brantley, 2010; Lebedeva and Brantley, 2017). The bedrock fracture spacing controls the initial boulder size (Fletcher and Brantley, 2010; Hausrath et al., 2011; Sklar et al., 2017), which also drives the rough weathering front progression of rock mass (Lebedeva and Brantley, 2017). A heterogeneous block size distribution is described depending on the balance between the resulting weathering and erosion rates (Brantley and Lebedeva, 2021).

Despite considerable progress on several fronts, the intricate couplings at stake with many physicochemical parameters (e.g., temperature, pH ,ionic strength,saturation index,water content) and mechanisms (evolution of reactive surface area with rock alteration, mineral armouring due to precipitation of insoluble secondary compounds) influencing chemical weathering rates (Anbeek, 1993; Emmanuel, 2022; Jung and Navarre-Sitchler, 2018b) represent a challenging task. Such difficulty is notably illustrated by the discrepancy observed between laboratory and field-measured dissolution rates with several orders of magnitude difference (Navarre-Sitchler and Brantley, 2007; White and Brantley, 2003), which reflects our inability to predict weathering rates at large scale. One of the main reasons behind this gap is the function of which limiting reaction mechanism prevails (kinetics limited or transport limited reaction). Laboratory experiments typically take place under well-stirred conditions and situated far from equilibrium, while typical field conditions exhibit a decrease in weathering rate over time due to the accumulation of dissolution products as the alteration front moves downward (Maher, 2010; Navarre-Sitchler and Thyne, 2007). Such observation has to be balanced in the presence of high erosion rates, which limit the reaction front extent and, hence, the evolution toward chemical equilibrium (Dixon et al., 2012; Schoonejans et al., 2016). Thus, kinetic-limited weathering is typically observed in actively eroding mountains (West et al., 2005), and the limiting mechanism is a good indicator of tectonic or climatic control on chemical weathering (Mao et al., 2022; Riebe et al., 2017). As an illustrative example, the weathering processes of peridotites in New Caledonia, which have resulted in laterite formation, are characterized by olivine dissolution under a hot, wet tropical climate with high erosion rates (Cathelineau et al., 2017; Teitler et al., 2019). Reactive transport simulations conducted by Favier et al. (2024) highlight that permeability heterogeneities control weathering propagation and olivine dissolution is driven by kinetics (see the calculations of olivine saturation index SI in Supporting Information S1 of Favier et al. (2024), as large negative values indicate that reaction occurs far from equilibrium). We will focus hereafter only on the kinetics dissolution regime, and the weathering of laterite supergene deposits in New Caledonia will be used for application purposes.

Pioneering works on dissolution processes in porous media (Golfier et al., 2002; Ladd and Szymczak, 2021; Szymczak and Ladd, 2006) and later in fractures (Deng and Peters, 2019; Detwiler et al., 2003; Detwiler and Rajaram, 2007; Elkhoury et al., 2013; Oltéan et al., 2013; Trinchero et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2022), have shed light on the influence of dimensionless numbers, respectively Péclet and Damköhler numbers,to predict dissolution patterns. At the field scale(10 - 100m), most weathering models have initially investigated the 1D vertical dissolution front (e.g., see Goddéris et al., 2019, for a general overview of these models). With the advances in modelling techniques of flow and transport in fractured mass rock (we refer the reader to the state-of-the-art review papers of MacQuarrie and Mayer, 2005, or Deng and Spycher, 2019) and computational power, however, recent numerical studies have attempted to explore this complex interplay between weathering processes and structural pathways in 2D and 3D fracture-matrix systems (e.g., Andrews et al., 2023; Andrews and Navarre-Sitchler, 2021; Favier et al., 2024; Jung and Navarre-Sitchler, 2018b, 2018a). Some results have emphasized the role of topography and lateral flows on weathering patterns in hills (Lebedeva and Brantley, 2017; Myagkiy et al., 2019), the competition between weathering and erosion and the impact of water table depth (Lebedeva and Brantley, 2020) or fracture densities (Lebedeva and Brantley, 2023). Simulations in the two-dimensional networks of Andrews and Navarre-Sitchler (2021) indicate that fractures act as fast flow paths through the rock mass where mineral dissolution remains driven by kinetics, while dissolution in the porous matrix occurs close to equilibrium and is limited by diffusion. Andrews et al. (2023) have extended these findings in three-dimensional fractured media. Steefel and Hu (2022) have shown how the weathering processes could affect the connectivity of a natural intersecting fracture network. Finally, simulations of Pandey and Rajaram (2016) and Favier et al. (2024) on Discrete Fracture Matrix (DFM) models have exhibited corestone features or boulders similar to the ones observed on the field. Both results highlight the role of the original fracture network on boulder sizes.

However, relationships between the boulder size distribution, the geometrical organization of fracture networks, and their hydrological characteristics have not yet been fully addressed. Indeed, depending on weathering fluids percolating through the fracture network, the weathering front geometry may be affected. More specifically, fracture networks tend to display a multiscale nature (Ehlen, 2000, 2002), which impacts flow properties, notably the network permeability at different scales (Davy et al., 2006; de Dreuzy et al., 2012). When multiple fracture sets are present, each one may have a different permeability, constraining the fluid flow. Thus, competition may occur between the different fracture sets. Highly permeable fractures channel the central part of fluid flow so that fluid circulation along the secondary network with less permeable discontinuities may be inhibited. To our knowledge, such competition has been studied in the context of flow patterns for oil recovery but never in the presence of dissolution (Gong and Rossen, 2018). This fluid-flow partitioning between fractures and rock matrix is exacerbated by the fluid-rock interactions which may occur. Therefore, this competition may result in different boulder size distributions depending on which fracture network is promoted relative to another Fig. 1).

This paper studies the impact of a geometrical organization (fracture spacing) and hydrological characteristics (permeability) of a multiscale fracture network composed of sub-orthogonal fractures on chemical weathering front propagation. The hydrogeochemical conditions under which a fracture network contributes to weathering at the expense of another are explored to predict better the dissolution pattern resulting from spheroidal weathering. The hydrogeochemical settings are first investigated in the light of dimensionless Péclet (Huysmans and Das-sargues, 2005) and Damköhler (Dijk et al., 2002) numbers, which describe the fluid flow and the chemical reaction regime, respectively. A simple case of a dual-scale fracture network was designed to study the impact of their variations on dissolution patterns. Then, the findings were upscaled to multiscale fracture networks using scaling laws. An application to lateritic deposits in New Caledonia was carried out to predict the boulder size observed at different scales. Indeed, local mining activity provides a rare opportunity to access laterite profiles developed after peridotite in which the fracture networks have been highly documented (Cathelineau et al., 2017; Favier et al., 2022; Iseppi et al., 2018; Jeanpert et al., 2019; Quesnel et al., 2016). Such application highlights the potentialities to predict highly variable-grade distribution resulting from weathering heterogeneities in supergene deposits.

2. First insight from a simple two-scale fracture network


A first analysis is conducted on a general simple dual-scale fracture network to highlight the physical mechanisms at stake. Different hydrological settings and dissolution rates were explored to reveal the conditions under which a secondary fracture network (F2) will constrain with the main network (F1), the boulder size, and conversely. Thus, the competition between two fracture sets, F1 and F2, with different per-meabilities, and the impact on boulder size formed from rock matrix dissolution was studied numerically.

The conceptual reactive transport model is intended to be as general as possible to tackle the broadest range of situations. Hence, a fictive simple geochemical system is studied (Eq. (4)). Bedrock is assumed to be dissolved by an acidic species A in solution coming from the upper left corner of the model (Fig. 2). The olivine dissolution has been studied extensively (Anbeek, 1993; Oelkers et al., 2018) and is used here as a proxy for various ul-tramafic rocks (Favier et al., 2022; Freyssinet and Farah, 2000; Tres-cases, 1975). For such conditions, the olivine dissolution rate is essentially constrained by pH variations (Oelkers et al.,2018; Wogelius and Walther, 1992), contrarily to transfer-limited reactions classically described from Transition State Theory. The simplicity of the geochemical model allows us to better highlight the impact of the fracture network properties, i.e. spacing and permeability, on the weathering regimes. Furthermore, despite its simplicity, such a model is consistent in a first-order approach, with more realistic geochemical models of mafic-to-ultramafic bedrock weathering (Myagkiy et al., 2017; Pokrovsky and Schott, 2000; Røyne et al., 2008). In addition, and for the sake of comparison, all results and variables will be presented as dimensionless, as defined in Section 2.1.2. Results will be thus interpreted in terms of governing dimensionless numbers to decipher the contribution of different mechanisms and physical parameters to the weathering pattern (Detwiler and Rajaram, 2007; Golfier et al., 2002). A more complex rate law of dissolution kinetics could eventually be analyzed by modifying the Damköhler number definition.

Using COMSOL Multiphysics software, we numerically solved the 2D problem (Eqs. (2) and (3)) with a finite element method. The effect of the flow regime on dissolution patterns is first investigated using a constant dissolution rate kA=10−7s−1 . Then,we study the impact of kinetics by testing variable dissolution kinetic rates ranging from 10−5s−1 to 10−9 s−1 for two different flow conditions.

2.2.1. Impact of flow regimes


Fig. 3 shows the different dissolution patterns obtained numerically from simulations and plotted in a Pe1−Pe2 diagram. As previously mentioned, the Péclet number represents the competition between diffusion and advection,so a high Pe corresponds to an advection-dominated configuration. Thus,at low Pe(Pe1<1 and Pe2<1) ,diffusion guides the rock dissolution (patterns 13 and 14). In contrast, patterns 10, 11 and 12 represent intermediate cases where the impact of advection, and thus of the F1 and F2 fracture networks on rock dissolution,begins to be visible. These patterns are observed for Pe between 1 and 10. Pattern 10 corresponds to a case where only F1 contributes to the weathering in addition to diffusion. Pattern 11 corresponds to an intermediate case where F2 starts to impact the dissolution, while for pattern 12, F2 drives dissolution. This gradation from F1 to F2 control is related to the increase of Pe2 . At a Pe1>10 and Pe2<1 ,the diffusion effect is no longer observed on the F1 network, so F1 solely drives the boulder shape. F2 begins to contribute to the boulder shape with F1 when Pe2 is comprised between 1 and 10 (patterns 3,6 and 7),and beyond Pe2=10 ,both F1 and F2 drive the dissolution pattern when Pe1 >10 (patterns 4 and 8).

Hence, to enhance the contribution of secondary network F2 at the expense of F1, a Pe higher than 10 is required. On the contrary, to constrain weathering patterns only by F1 at the expense of F2 ,a Pe2 lower than 1 is required.

Three main weathering regimes can be thus identified based on this analysis:

Diffusion-dominated weathering regime (regime I, Pe1<1 and Pe2<1 ,representative pattern: 14): Diffusion mechanism dominates over transport by convection, and instabilities cannot develop. A uniform dissolution front is observed.

Primary fracture-dominated weathering regime (regime II, Pe1> 10 and Pe2<1 ; representative pattern: 1): Mass transport in fractures F1 is driven by advection, but diffusion-dominated flow still occurs in fractures F2. A shrinking core mechanism reveals a boulder-shaped dissolution pattern delineated by the F1 fracture network (Favier et al., 2024; Pandey and Rajaram, 2016).

Secondary fracture-dominated weathering regime (regime III, Pe1>10 and Pe2>10 ; representative pattern: 4): Mass transport in both fracture sets is dominated by advection. Progression of the dissolution front results in a boulder-shaped dissolution pattern delineated by both the F1 and F2 fracture networks. The complete imprint of the original fracture network is preserved.

A transitional regime between the main weathering regimes is also identified (regime IV). Different representative patterns(6,11,13)are observed depending on the transition between the main regimes.

Note that boundaries between the different regimes have been drawn based on a qualitative observation of the dissolution patterns, and some uncertainties are associated with the transition regions. Fig. 3 highlights that the transition between the different dissolution regimes is only driven by the values of the two Péclet numbers and does not depend on the Damköhler number. A similar finding has been found for wormholing regimes in homogeneous porous media (Golfier et al., 2002). In conclusion, the changes in fracture permeability through increased hydraulic aperture drive the dissolution pattern and, thus, the boulder size.

2.2.2. Impact of reaction kinetics


Fig. 4 shows the different dissolution patterns obtained numerically by varying Da at constant Péclet number. Two representative values of Pe2 are considered,1 (regime II) and 10 (regime III),respectively,while Pe1 is kept constant and equal at 500 . In regime II,only the primary fracture network is expressed,and Da1 is considered for analysis,while in regime III,both fractures are represented,and Da2 is preferentially calculated. A similar Da value between the two regimes does not necessarily correspond to the same reaction rate value. Indeed, in Regime III (pattern 7),for instance, Da1≈400 corresponds to a rate kA= 5⋅10−8s−1 ,while the same Da2 value is obtained in regime II for kA=10−8 s−1 . However,both patterns are remarkably similar,illustrating the competition between the characteristic times of diffusion and reaction kinetics. Consequently,we do not differentiate Da1 and Da2 in Fig. 4.

We observe that Da variations induced by changes in dissolution rate mainly influence the size of the dissolution halo but not the weathering regime itself (Fig. 4), as explained in Li et al. (2014). A high Da reduces the size of the dissolution halo and favors the formation of well-delineated patterns,whereas a low Da favors a more diffuse dissolution of the whole rock. Indeed,for Da≫1 ,the reaction rate is far greater than the diffusion rate. The reaction is thus assumed to occur instantaneously at the boulder rim, creating a sharp transition with the undissolved boulder part. On the other hand,for Da close to 1,reaction and diffusion co-occur. Hence, the dissolution is more uniform within all the boulders. Such results are consistent with previous studies regarding the impact of the Da and the reaction rates on the width of the reaction front (Genthon et al., 2017; Genthon and Ormond, 2008; Li et al., 2014; Panga et al., 2005; Szymczak and Ladd, 2009).

3. Weathering front in a multiscale fracture network: application to boulder size prediction in New Caledonia


3.1.A theoretical weathering model in fractured porous media


The simple case study of the two-scale fracture network highlights the influence of the fluid flow regime on fracture-driven weathering. We observed that this dependency can be expressed solely through the Péclet number. More explicitly,for Pe>10 ,the fracture set contributes to weathering. Assuming a similar relationship can be observed across different scales ranging between a lower and an upper cutoff scale, we may predict the large-scale chemical weathering features and boulder formation. Indeed, the fluid flow regime in fracture networks is notably driven by the fracture spacing and their hydraulic aperture. It is noteworthy that these parameters tend to follow scaling laws (Davy et al., 2006; de Dreuzy et al., 2012), classically expressed as power-law distributions, such as

(7)ai(l)=αlb

where α is a constant, b is a power-law exponent and l[m] is the observation scale of interest.

(2.b) and the cubic law given in Eq. 3.2. Application to laterite deposits in New Caledonia


3.2.1. Geological description


New Caledonia is an archipelago located in SW Pacific (Fig. 5). Ul-tramafic rocks account for about 30% of the exposed surface of the main "Grande Terre" island, as relics of the Peridotite Nappe obducted during the Eocene period (Cluzel et al. 2001). After obduction, the Peridotite Nappe underwent deep weathering under a hot and wet tropical climate, resulting in laterite formation (Sevin et al., 2014; Maurizot et al., 2020). The development of lateritic deposits is caused by the leaching of soluble elements(Mg,Ni,Si)from the peridotite bedrock. Dissolved elements reprecipitate deeper in the saprolite horizon as newly formed phyllosi-licates and Fe-oxy-hydroxides. Finally, the newly formed phyllosilicates are also dissolved to enable the formation of an oxide-rich horizon (Butt and Cluzel, 2013; Cathelineau et al., 2017; Dublet et al., 2012; Freys-sinet et al., 2005; Golightly, 2010; Haldar, 2017; Santoro et al., 2022; Teitler et al., 2019). Weathering is driven by permeability heterogeneities and a dense fracture network (Favier et al., 2022, 2024; Iseppi et al., 2018; Jeanpert et al., 2019).

Due to the intense fracturing of bedrock at different scales (Favier et al., 2022; Iseppi et al., 2018), weathering heterogeneities such as boulders in the saprolite zone (Myagkiy et al., 2019) are observed from the deposit scale to the sample scale (Fig. 1, Fig. 6). Boulders are characterized by a concentric weathering zonation: the outer part is more weathered than the core (Myagkiy et al., 2019).

At each scale, they display an evenly spaced distribution so that a scaling law of their size can be stated by measuring the distance between the centre of neighbouring boulders or, when visible enough, between the delineating fractures (Fig. 6).

3.2.2. Scaling of fracture network


Fractured rock masses exhibit inherent spatial variability with different levels of complexity at various scales, which are complex to consider in flow or geomechanical modelling (Ma et al., 2022b, 2022a). 3.3. Boulder size prediction on a multiscale fracture network


Before applying our theoretical weathering model to geological observations, we must assess the applicability at any scale of the Pe threshold value required for promoting the boulder delineation. For this purpose, we extend our model to a three-scale fracture network and simulate the formation of the boulders in the presence of three fracture sets, F1, F2, and F3, yielding different aperture and spacing values. Fracture spacings and permeabilities (i.e. apertures) are derived from the previously described scaling laws (Fig. 7). Note that this dependency implies that fracture spacing, aperture and permeability are linked. In other words, a densely spaced fracture network will display a high permeability. Different weathering patterns have been simulated over a domain of 20×15m (patterns 1−4 ,as identified in Fig. 8),and results illustrate the competition between the fracture networks.

The simulation is conducted on COMSOL Multiphysics, with the same hydrogeochemical settings as the dual-scale fracture network case study. The only difference with the numerical conditions illustrated in Fig. 2 lies in the fracture network geometry and the 0.7m/m hydraulic gradient corresponding to the median hydraulic gradient in the Koniambo massif (Jeanpert, 2017). The gradient is applied at the upper boundary of the model, while the lower boundary has an outflow condition. A periodic condition is used on the other sides of the domain.

Results support previous results as the contribution of any fracture is controlled by the value of Pe (Fig. 8). In pattern 1,a weathered bedrock is created where only F1 participates in boulder delineation. Perme-abilities of F2 and F3, which have, in that case, the same spacing, are too small to reach a Péclet number of 10 (Fig. 8 and Fig. 9). Pattern 2 represents a fractured medium where both F1 and F2 participate in weathering. F1 boulders cluster F2 boulders, while F3 is not expressed. In pattern 3, all fracture networks are represented in agreement with their Pe above 10. Large boulder clusters are delineated by F1,inside which smaller clusters are observed, delimited by F2, which are themselves delineated into boulder clusters by F3. Pattern 4 is an extreme case where F1 is much larger than F2 and F3, which exhibit the same geometrical features. Hence, the large boulders shaped by F1 are then cut into smaller ones by F2 and F3, which belong to the same network. These patterns are also consistent with the spatial organisation of the boulders encountered on the field. For example, pattern 2 (Fig. 8) mimics the outcrop structure described in Fig. 1. A primary fracture network F1 (white dashed lines in Fig. 1) clusters boulders delineated by the secondary fracture network F2 (yellow dashed lines in Fig. 1).

A clustering of boulders is indeed observed based on the permeability and spacing of the fracture network. Large fracture networks are characterized by high permeability. Thus,a high Pe delineates large boulders, divided by smaller fracture networks characterized by lower permeability and Pe ,into clusters of smaller boulders. Such phenomenon is repeated successively for each fracture network whose Pe is above 10 .

3.5. Estimation of the minimum size of boulders in the massifs of New Caledonia


Since no information on the paleo-hydraulic gradient that prevailed during the formation of laterites is available, the current vertical hydraulic gradient value is used. Indeed, in the early stages of formation, weathering is assumed to be controlled by vertical infiltration before uplift and promotion of lateral flow through increased matrix permeability (Butt and Cluzel, 2013; Dewandel et al., 2017; Jeanpert, 2017). The current hydraulic gradient probably overestimates the paleo-gradient because of the permeability increase due to weathering. Hence,the evolution of Pei as a function of fracture spacing is plotted for the minimum and the maximum vertical gradient measured in all massifs to account for uncertainties. As numerical simulations on discrete fracture networks show, the fracture will enhance weathering front propagation if the corresponding Péclet number is higher than 10. For Pe between 1 and 10,we should observe an intermediate pattern between a non-delineated network and the onset of boulders linked to the competition with diffusion (Fig. 10).

Therefore,such constraints on the Pei value give a minimum boulder size expected for the upper and lower bounds of the gradient. Interestingly, the higher the hydraulic gradient, the smaller the minimum boulder size (Table 3).

These results are globally coherent with the field measurements (Fig. 6) regarding the different hypotheses and simplifications made. Boulders at the sample scale range from 5 to 10cm and are poorly delimited (Fig. 6.c). Hence,the Pe should be close to 1,roughly in agreement with boulder sizes obtained at Pe=1 for high hydraulic gradients such as in Koniambo. Boulder sizes at Pe=10 tend to be larger than those measured on the field at the outcrop scale, where well-delimited boulders range from 15 to 30cm (Fig. 6.b). As a higher hydraulic gradient leads to smaller boulders, we infer that boulders observed on the field should have formed under a higher hydraulic gradient than the value used in our prediction. Furthermore, it is possible to assume local variations in the hydraulic gradient, either from the topographic effect or the medium permeability (Dewandel et al., 2017), that cause the size of boulders to vary locally.

3.6. Sensitivity of the current approach


If the current approach allows deriving scaling laws to predict boulder size and organization, it is highly sensitive to the quality of the estimated scaling laws. The subjective choice of the permeability and fracture spacing observation scale can strongly affect the scaling laws. Furthermore, the scaling laws only apply to a limited range of scales that should be more precisely investigated. In addition, such an approach assumes that the permeability of porous fractured medium is only driven by fractures at all the investigated scales. Previous studies also highlight the difficulties of upscaling the flow properties from the fracture scale to the network scale due to the roughness of fractures and the network topology (de Dreuzy et al., 2012).

This method is also sensitive to the estimates of the hydraulic gradient. Indeed, as discussed above, the hydraulic gradient is highly variable in New Caledonia depending on the season and the presence of highly conductive discontinuities, and it is likely to have changed through geological times since the onset of weathering.

Regarding the geochemical aspect, the chemical model used for this study is reduced to a highly simplistic system of first-order dissolution for clarity. Yet, more complex systems with cycles of dissolution-reprecipitation may change the behaviour of the weathering front progression, as well as the porosity-permeability changes of the rock as a function of mineralogical changes.

4. Conclusions


This study investigates the impact of hydro-geochemical features on chemical weathering front propagation in fractured bedrock. Competition between multiple fracture networks results in different dissolution patterns and variable-size boulder formation. This work focuses on geological systems characterized by high erosion rates and kinetic-limited weathering, such as supergene deposits in New Caledonia. The geometry of the computational domain relies on idealized structures of fracture networks and simplified geochemistry. Still, we use this simplification to conduct a sensitivity analysis of physical parameters governing fluid flow and weathering processes. Numerical modeling accounting for two and three-fracture networks has shed light on the influence of these fluid flow and reaction rate conditions, assessed through the dimensionless numbers Pe and Da ,on dissolution patterns. Despite its simplicity, the proposed conceptual model is numerically validated and confronted against in situ observations from lateritic deposits in New Caledonia. The primary outcomes can be summarized as follows:

A fracture network will contribute to delineating the boulder shape if the associated fluid flow regime reaches a Pe threshold value of around 10. On the contrary, predominant diffusion will inhibit this fracture set in favor of a higher permeability fracture network. The threshold value is reached when the network permeability is high enough. Therefore, fracture permeability, network connectivity, and spacing are important parameters to consider when discussing the contribution of a given fracture network (de Dreuzy et al., 2012; Long and Witherspoon, 1985) to weathering and, thus, the shape of dissolution patterns.

In contrast, the Damköhler number controls the dissolution halo width in the boulder. For Da≈1 ,a uniform dissolution front is observed, while for Da>104 ,a thin dissolution halo occurs. Such results are consistent with those obtained in the case of wormholes (Barker and Dipple, 2019; Cabeza et al., 2020), where an optimum dissolution regime can be predicted in a Pe-Da diagram (Golfier et al., 2002). An optimum Da value, Daopt  ,maximizing the weathering rate of porous fractured bedrock, should also exist in our simulations and will be investigated in further studies.

The dependency of the fluid flow regime on the fracture network characteristics (fracture permeability and spacing) can be expressed using scaling laws. We thus succeeded in estimating the size of the boulders at different scales by predicting the fracture contribution as a function of their Pe. Hence, any fracture network displaying a fracture spacing above the spacing threshold corresponding to a Pe of 10 can generate boulders. Those boulders are then clustered hierarchically according to the size of their developing fracture network. As a consequence, the boulder size distribution and spatial organization are not only controlled by topological effects and competition between weathering and erosion rates (Brantley et al., 2011; Fletcher and Brantley, 2010; Lebedeva and Brantley, 2013, 2017) but also by the competition between the inherited fracture networks.

Furthermore, such a conceptual model may have implications for mineral ore targeting. Indeed, the intensity of weathering controls Ni-grade distribution in lateritic deposits (Cathelineau et al., 2016; Myag-kiy et al., 2017). Hence, by better predicting the boulder size distribution and spatial organization, such an approach may help to identify the specific localization of Ni -hotspots,which are of prime interest to mining companies.
